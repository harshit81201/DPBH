Welcome to Dark Patterns Awareness

This is your go-to resource for understanding and reporting dark patterns. Here, you can learn about what dark patterns are, how they work, and their impact on cybersecurity.

Dark patterns are design strategies that manipulate user behavior to achieve the goals of the website or application designer, often at the expense of user interests. These patterns exploit cognitive biases or employ deceptive tactics to trick users into performing actions they might not otherwise choose to do.

Examples of Dark Patterns:

  • Misdirection: Guiding users towards unintended choices by distracting or confusing them.
  • Forced Continuity: Automatically renewing subscriptions without clear notification or making it difficult to cancel.
  • Hidden Costs: Concealing additional charges until the user is committed to a purchase.
  • Sneak into Basket: Adding extra items to the shopping cart without the user's explicit consent.
  • Roach Motel: Making it easy to get into a situation but difficult to get out of it (e.g., signing up for a service).
  • Confirmshaming: Guilt-tripping users into opting into certain actions by making the alternative seem socially unacceptable.
  • Privacy Zuckering: Tricking users into sharing more information than they intended, named after Facebook's CEO, Mark Zuckerberg.
  • Bait and Switch: Promising a certain outcome or feature but delivering something else entirely.

These patterns can undermine trust between users and the platforms they interact with, leading to negative user experiences and potential damage to the organization's reputation. Consequently, many advocate for transparent and ethical design practices that prioritize user empowerment and honesty.

How Dark Patterns Work:

Dark patterns take advantage of human psychology to subtly coerce users into making certain choices. Below is an overview of how they operate:

  • Confusing Navigation: Designed to disorient or lead users on a different path than they intended, often resulting in accidental purchases or subscriptions.
  • Disguised Ads: Advertisements that are made to look like regular content or navigation, tricking users into clicking on them.
  • Forced Action: Requiring users to perform a specific action, like signing up for a newsletter, in order to access certain functionalities or continue with their intended task.
  • Friend Spam: The product asks for your email or social media permissions under the guise of a benefit and then spams your contacts without clear consent.
  • Hidden Information: Important information is hidden or only revealed at the last moment, which can lead to unexpected charges or terms.
